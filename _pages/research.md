<br><br>**Attention, L2 proficiency, and interference between tempo and vowel quality convergence**<br>
Previous work investigating the phenomenon of phonetic convergence has used a variety of experimental paradigms, with some designed to elicit spontaneous speech in the context of a conversation and others eliciting individual word forms in non-interactional contexts, in order to probe the circumstances under which speakers’ productions become more similar to that of an interlocuter or model talker, as well as to what degree this may be modulated by various factors. The current study extends this question to L2 speech production by investigating phonetic convergence in L1 English - L2 French learners across a range of proficiency levels to explore the degree to which L2 proficiency interacts with attentional factors to modulate transient and persistent phonetic convergence. Convergence to both segmental and prosodic dimensions are examined, specifically vowel quality, articulation rate, and speech rate. Using an explicit imitation paradigm with different elicitation instructions (“repeat”, “imitate”) to operationalize attention to phonetic detail, this study finds evidence of convergence to a model talker in articulation rate but divergence in speech rate, as well as an interference between tempo and vowel quality convergence, which is in turn modulated by proficiency in the L2.
